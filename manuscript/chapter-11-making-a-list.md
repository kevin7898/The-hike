# Chapter 11 - Making A List

Gear lists made excellent camouflage.

Kelsey discovered that over the next week. A person could text another person at 8:17 in the morning about sock liners and not be flirting. She could send a picture of three dehydrated meal options arranged on her kitchen counter and ask which tasted least like salted regret. She could debate water-filter brands, pack weight, trail snacks, bear hangs, bug spray, and whether Sam's definition of "light packing" included a full-size hairbrush.

All of that was normal.

The fact that Kevin answered quickly was also normal. He liked preparation. He liked gear. He had opinions about rain covers that bordered on moral philosophy.

`Garbage bag liner inside the pack matters more than the pack cover.`

Kelsey answered, `That sounds like something you say before revealing a bunker.`

`A dry sleeping bag is civilisation.`

`Sam knows you talk like this?`

`Sam benefits from my infrastructure.`

Five minutes later, Sam sent a picture of a pile on the bedroom floor: leggings, two sweaters, a paperback, three pairs of socks, a toiletry bag, and what appeared to be a small throw blanket.

`Infrastructure review please.`

Kevin:

`No.`

Sam:

`Oppression.`

Kelsey laughed at her desk hard enough that one of her coworkers looked over the divider.

The trip was still officially Sam and Kevin's. Kelsey had not been invited into this specific one in a way that required an answer, and yet she was somehow involved in every planning conversation. That was Sam's doing, mostly. Sam sent questions to the group chat with the theatrical helplessness of a person who knew exactly how competent she was and preferred to outsource anyway.

`How many underwears does wilderness require?`

Kelsey typed, `That depends how annoying Kevin is.`

Kevin:

`I am not responsible for underwear math.`

Sam:

`Coward.`

Kelsey stared at the word underwear on her phone longer than it deserved.

Kevin did not make it weird.

That was becoming one of his most inconvenient qualities.

On Thursday evening, Kelsey spread her own gear across the living room floor even though she had no confirmed reason to do so. Tent. Pad. Quilt. Stove. Rain shell. Fleece. Base layers. Headlamp, now with new batteries. Knife. Mug. First-aid kit. Water filter. Dry socks rolled inside a dry bag because Kevin had made a convincing argument about civilisation.

Her phone buzzed.

Kevin:

`Do you have trekking poles?`

She looked at the closet, where one pole leaned alone because its partner had vanished during a move.

`I have trekking pole. Singular. He is lonely but brave.`

`Borrow mine if you want.`

`What will you use?`

`I rarely use them unless the descent is bad.`

`Your knees are younger than your personality.`

`Sam says that is libel.`

Kelsey smiled and sat back on her heels.

Then Kevin sent another message.

`Also, if you come on an overnight with us, we can split some shared weight. You don't need to duplicate everything.`

If.

Not when.

Not this one.

Still, the sentence opened space.

Kelsey wrote, deleted, wrote again.

`Good to know. I have not committed to joining your wilderness bureaucracy.`

`Understood. The forms remain available.`

She put the phone down and folded her fleece with unnecessary precision.

The next day, Kevin stopped by her house after work to drop off a spare dry bag and the extra trekking poles. Sam was supposed to come too, but a staff meeting ran long and then longer in the way meetings did when no one respected daylight.

Kelsey almost told Kevin to leave the gear on the porch.

Instead, she opened the door.

He stood there in work clothes, tired around the eyes, holding poles in one hand and a blue dry bag in the other.

"Delivery," he said.

"Is there a service fee?"

"You have to tell Sam I was helpful."

"She won't believe me."

"Then exaggerate."

Kelsey stepped back. "Come in for a minute."

He did.

Her house felt different with him inside it. Not wrong. Just newly witnessed. The shoes by the door, the blanket over the couch, the mug in the sink, the gear spread across the living room like she was preparing for an expedition instead of avoiding her own thoughts.

Kevin looked around without making her feel inspected.

"Good view," he said, nodding toward the deck.

"Better when the neighbour's hedge admits defeat."

"Long campaign?"

"Years."

He set the gear beside her pack and crouched to look at what she had laid out. "You are more prepared than Sam."

"Everyone is more prepared than Sam."

"Sam is prepared emotionally."

"For soup?"

"For demanding soup."

Kelsey sat on the arm of the couch while he checked the dry bag's closure and showed her how the trekking pole locks adjusted. His hands were practical and sure. He did not draw attention to them. That made it worse.

"Do I need these?" she asked.

"Need? No. But if the trail is wet, they help. Especially coming down with weight."

"You always think about coming down."

He glanced up. "Most accidents happen when people think the hard part is over."

The answer was so Kevin that it knocked the innuendo out of her head and replaced it with something warmer.

"That is very you," she said.

"Bleak and practical?"

"Annoyingly considerate."

He looked at her then. Really looked, not long enough to become a challenge, but long enough to acknowledge the line she had stepped near.

"I can live with that," he said.

Her pulse moved.

Before either of them could decide what came next, Sam called.

Kelsey answered on speaker because cowardice sometimes looked exactly like loyalty.

"Is he being weird in your house?" Sam demanded.

Kevin sat back on his heels. "I am providing equipment support."

"That sounds like being weird."

"Your dry bag has holes," he said.

"Do not bring facts into this."

Kelsey laughed, and the room returned to a shape she recognized.

After Kevin left, she carried the trekking poles to the closet, then brought them back out and set them beside her pack.

Not committed.

Not yet.

But the list in her phone had changed. The trip no longer felt like Sam and Kevin's plan that Kelsey was helping with from the edge.

It felt like a door someone kept leaving open on purpose.
