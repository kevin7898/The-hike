# Chapter 29 - Lake-Safe Soap

Day three began with a deadline.

Kelsey woke to Kevin unzipping the tent door quietly enough to fail. Pale light filled the mesh. Cold air slipped in around the quilt and found every place her sleep clothes had twisted. Her body objected to consciousness in several languages at once: calves from the mountain, hips from the sleeping pad, thighs from Kevin, shoulders from the pack waiting to be carried out.

"No," she said into the pillow.

Kevin paused halfway out of the tent. "No to morning?"

"Correct."

"Understandable."

"Bring back civilization."

"Coffee?"

"That is a start."

He smiled and disappeared.

The campsite sounded different on the last morning. Less like shelter, more like inventory. Stove clicking. Water poured. A stuff sack rustling. The lake below camp moving with insulting calm. Kelsey lay still and let memory arrange itself: the first cold plunge, the ruined tent, the fire, Kevin's hand, Sam's text, the summit blanket, the island, the dark conversation about what she had liked.

Leaving made all of it feel fragile.

If they packed the tent, did the version of them inside it get packed too?

She hated the thought immediately for being dramatic and also not wrong.

Kevin returned with coffee and the expression of a man prepared to negotiate with a hostile government.

"Offering," he said.

Kelsey took the mug from inside the quilt. "Accepted."

He sat just outside the tent door. "We have time, but not endless time."

"That is a rude category."

"If we break camp efficiently, we can wash properly before packing out."

Kelsey opened one eye. "Properly in a lake?"

"Lake-safe soap. Away from shore for lather, rinse with carried water, no residue in the lake."

"You have a system for being naked and responsible."

"I contain multitudes."

She smiled into the coffee.

They did not have one last dramatic morning in the tent. Kelsey had expected to want that, or to feel she should want it, as proof that the trip's heat had not faded. Instead, what she wanted was slower and more ordinary. Kevin sitting beside her while she drank coffee. His hand warm around her ankle under the quilt. A kiss that tasted like sleep and camp coffee. The way he asked, "Body okay?" and waited for the actual answer.

"Tender," she said. "Good tender."

"No sex tender?"

She laughed. "Mostly."

"Useful distinction."

"Do you want a final sex montage for your records?"

"I want you not to hike out miserable because we got sentimental."

That went straight through her.

"You are annoyingly good at this," she said.

"At what?"

"Making restraint feel wanted."

His thumb moved once over her ankle. "I do want you."

"I know."

"Good."

The words were enough.

They kissed for a while in the half-open tent, slow and warm under the quilt, hands familiar but not escalating past what her body could happily carry. Kevin touched her because she leaned into it. She touched him because she wanted one more memory of his breath changing under her hand. They stopped before stopping felt like denial.

That, too, felt like something learned.

Then came the work.

Sleeping bags shaken out. Pads deflated with undignified wheezing. Clothes sorted into damp, smoky, wearable, and crimes against smell. Trash counted. The fire ring checked and scattered cold. Food bag lowered. Kevin's ruined tent lashed more securely for the hike out, because the moldy disgrace still had to come home.

Sam found brief signal somehow before they did.

When Kelsey checked her phone near the shore and saw one bar flicker alive, a message arrived.

`Morning check. Still love you. Still nosy. Bring details and do not leave garbage.`

Kelsey laughed and sent back:

`Still alive. Still sore. No garbage. Details pending.`

Sam:

`Proud on all counts.`

Kevin read it over her shoulder with permission and smiled.

"She is going to be unbearable," he said.

"She has earned some."

"True."

The lake bath came last, after most of camp was packed but before they changed into hiking clothes. Kevin carried water in the collapsible basin to a flat spot well back from shore. The soap was unscented, biodegradable, and used sparingly because Kevin had opinions about not contaminating water sources that Kelsey respected even while shivering.

"This is the least sexy hygiene ritual ever," she said, standing in camp sandals and a towel.

Kevin looked at her bare shoulder, the towel tucked above her breasts, the bite of cold making her skin pebble.

"Not least."

"Do not make environmental compliance hot."

"I am trying not to."

She laughed and handed him the soap.

They washed in turns and then together in the clumsy way of people who had become intimate enough for practicality to feel tender. Kevin poured water over her hair while she bent forward, careful not to let suds run toward the lake. Kelsey washed smoke from his neck, dirt from his forearm, the dried salt of sweat from his shoulder. The touch was not neutral. It did not become sex. That made it more intimate in a different direction.

When he knelt to pour clean water over her calves, she touched his wet hair.

"You do not have to take care of me every second."

He looked up. "I know."

"Do you?"

"Yes." He squeezed water from the cloth. "This is not because I think you are fragile. This is because I like being allowed."

Kelsey had to look away.

"That was another unfairly good answer."

"I am having a strong weekend."

"Do not get smug."

"Too late."

The cold rinse at the lake edge was brutal and funny. They took turns stepping in just far enough to splash clean water over feet and ankles, laughing through clenched teeth. Kelsey slipped once and grabbed Kevin's arm. He caught her, because of course he did, and she kissed him right there with cold water around their calves and sunlight breaking through cloud.

The kiss tasted like the end of something and the refusal to make it final.

They dressed in hiking clothes over clean, chilled skin. Kelsey chose practical underwear for the hike out, and the choice felt like care rather than retreat. The red pair, washed carefully and dry enough, went into the laundry bag with the rest of the evidence.

Before they left, they walked the campsite twice.

No wrappers. No food scraps. Fire dead. Tent pad brushed clean. Rocks returned. Kevin checked the branch where the food bag had hung. Kelsey checked the shore where their wet clothes had dripped the day before. The place looked like it had before them, which was the point and somehow sad.

Kevin shouldered his pack and the additional shame bundle of the ruined tent.

Kelsey lifted hers, grimaced at the weight, and settled the hip belt.

"Ready?" he asked.

She looked at the lake.

No. Yes.

"Almost."

She walked down to the water one last time. The surface moved under a light wind. Across the lake, the mountain held its shape in cloud shadow. Somewhere up there was a flat rock, a folded blanket memory, and a version of Kelsey who had chosen daylight.

Kevin came to stand beside her.

"Leaving feels weird," she said.

"Yeah."

"Not bad."

"No."

"Just... I don't want this to become something we only knew how to be here."

He turned toward her. "Then we make sure it doesn't."

Simple. Not easy. Enough.

Kelsey took his hand. Held it in open view of no one but the lake.

Then she let go, adjusted her pack, and started toward the trail.
