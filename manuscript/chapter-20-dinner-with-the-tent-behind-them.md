# Chapter 20 - Dinner With The Tent Behind Them

Dinner was not romantic enough to save them.

It involved a stove that took three tries to light, a packet of rice that claimed to serve two and meant two people with disappointing appetites, and Kevin dropping the clean spoon into dirt less than a minute after explaining his system for keeping utensils clean. Kelsey laughed so hard she had to sit down on the flat rock beside the stove.

"This is why Sam calls you infrastructure," she said.

Kevin rinsed the spoon with filtered water, wounded. "Infrastructure occasionally suffers setbacks."

"Infrastructure dropped the spoon."

"Infrastructure is aware."

They ate from separate bowls with their knees almost touching and the tent behind them in the trees. The lake held the last of the light. Smoke from a small, careful fire shifted with the wind and made Kelsey's fleece smell like the trip would linger in her laundry for days.

Hunger helped at first. So did the simple work of food: stirring, passing salt, burning her tongue, laughing when Kevin produced a chocolate bar from a pocket like a man revealing state secrets. For fifteen minutes, the day became only the day. Trail, water, camp, food, tired legs.

Then the bowls were empty.

The tent remained.

Kevin leaned back against a log, one arm resting on his raised knee. Firelight moved over his face in uneven colour. He looked tired in a way that made him less guarded, and Kelsey had to look away before the softness of it made her reckless.

"What are you thinking?" he asked.

"That I am going to smell like smoke forever."

"True, but not the whole answer."

She dragged the toe of her sandal through dirt. "I am thinking that Sam is probably pretending not to check her phone every ten minutes."

Kevin's expression softened. "Yeah."

"Do you feel bad?"

"Yes."

The answer landed cleanly.

Kelsey nodded because she did too.

"But not only bad," he said.

She looked at him.

"I miss her," Kevin continued. "I am grateful she made room. I am worried about her. I want to text her. I also want to be here with you without turning her into the person we use to avoid naming what is happening."

Kelsey stared at the fire until the flames blurred.

"That was a lot."

"Too much?"

"No. Just... accurate."

He let the silence hold it.

Kelsey pulled her sleeves over her hands. "I keep thinking I should feel guilt in one clean way. Like if I were a better friend, I would either not want this or I would want it with perfect confidence because she said it was okay."

"And instead?"

"Instead I want it and I am scared and I love her and I trust you and sometimes all of that fits together for three seconds, and then it doesn't."

Kevin looked at the fire.

"That sounds familiar."

"Does it?"

"Different pieces. Same weather."

She smiled faintly. "You and your weather."

"Island man."

The joke barely covered the tenderness under it.

Kelsey set her bowl aside and wrapped both arms around her knees. "What did Sam's note say to you? Not the tent part."

Kevin took a breath. "She told me not to be so careful that I make you feel unwanted."

Kelsey's body reacted before her thoughts did. Heat under skin. A small ache low in her stomach. The memory of him saying, You look very good.

"Oh," she said.

"Yeah."

"Are you?"

"Trying not to be."

"Careful?"

"So careful it becomes its own kind of dishonesty."

She looked at him across the little fire. "And what would honest look like?"

Kevin's jaw moved once. "Telling you that I have wanted to touch you for weeks."

Everything in the campsite seemed to sharpen. Fire crackle. Wind in the branches. The faint slap of water on rock below them.

Kelsey's breath left slowly.

"Where?"

The word surprised them both.

Kevin did not answer quickly. That was good. The pause made the answer feel chosen rather than hungry.

"Your hand first," he said.

Her fingers tightened around her sleeves.

"That is very restrained."

"It is where I keep starting."

She felt the memory of every offered hand over roots and rocks, every release that happened half a second before she wanted it to.

"And after that?"

His eyes held hers. "Your waist. Your back where your shirt rides up under the pack. Your mouth when you make a joke because you are nervous."

Kelsey stopped breathing properly.

Kevin's face changed. "Too much?"

"No."

"Kels."

"No," she said again, stronger. "I asked."

The honesty steadied both of them and made the air hotter.

She could have answered with her own list. His shoulders on the descent. His throat when he drank. His hands on knots and straps and camera controls. The way he looked away at the lake because he wanted to be good and wanted her more.

She was not ready to say all of it.

"I want you to touch me," she said instead.

Kevin did not move.

"Not everything," she added, because precision mattered. "Not because I said one sentence and now the whole night has to happen. But I want to stop pretending I am only accidentally near you."

His breath shifted. "Okay."

"Okay?"

"I want that too."

The fire popped, throwing a brief spray of sparks upward. Kelsey flinched, and the ordinary world returned just enough for both of them to laugh shakily.

Kevin stood, came around the fire, and sat beside her on the log. Not touching at first. Close enough that warmth moved between their sleeves.

"Hand first?" he asked.

The question might have sounded silly from anyone else.

From Kevin, it made her throat tight.

Kelsey put her hand palm-up on the log between them.

He laid his hand over hers.

That was all. Skin, warmth, callus at the base of his fingers, his thumb settling carefully against the side of her hand. No rush. No performance. The entire day seemed to gather itself into that contact: the truck, the trail, the lake, the ruined tent, the underwear, the fire, Sam's handwriting folded in his pack.

Kelsey turned her hand and laced their fingers together.

Kevin looked at her mouth.

She saw it.

The choice came up through her body before fear could organize itself.

"Not yet," she whispered.

His fingers tightened once around hers. "Okay."

He did not hide disappointment. He did not make it her problem.

That made her lean into his shoulder.

They sat that way until the fire burned lower and the cold began moving in from the lake. The tent behind them stopped feeling like a threat. It felt like a question waiting for the person who had the right to answer.

Kelsey was not ready yet.

But she was closer than she had been before dinner.
