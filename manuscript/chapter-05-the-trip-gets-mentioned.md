# Chapter 5 - The Trip Gets Mentioned

The camping trip entered the conversation while Sam was sitting on a log, eating trail mix and accusing raisins of ruining democracy.

"They are not candy," she said, holding one between two fingers like evidence. "They are lies with wrinkles."

Kevin took the bag from her. "You bought that mix."

"Under capitalism, all choices are compromised."

Kelsey was too busy catching her breath to help. They had climbed harder than Sam had promised, which was exactly as hard as Kevin had warned and nowhere near as casual as the group text had implied. The trail above Nanaimo River was dry in the exposed sections and slick under trees, all dust, roots, and sudden dips that made conversation break into practical warnings.

The three of them had fallen into an order without discussing it. Kevin ahead on the steeper parts, because he spotted bad footing early. Sam in the middle, complaining for sport but climbing well. Kelsey behind them sometimes, beside Sam other times, taking in the shift of muscle under backpacks and the shine of sweat at everyone's temples as the day warmed.

It was a hiking day first. That mattered.

Kelsey liked the work of it: the heat building under her shirt, the way her lungs opened after the first miserable twenty minutes, the hard-earned view when the trees broke and the river flashed below. She liked Sam's dramatic suffering and Kevin's quiet preparedness. She liked stopping for water and feeling the world narrow to shade, thirst, and the need to retie a boot before the next climb.

So when Sam mentioned the overnight, Kelsey reacted as a hiker.

"Two nights?" she asked.

"Maybe," Sam said. "If the weather holds and Kevin stops describing elevation gain like it is a personality test."

Kevin leaned against a cedar, one strap of his pack loosened. "It is not a personality test."

"You said the phrase 'good sustained climb' with affection."

"That is a neutral description."

"It is a threat wearing hiking pants."

Kelsey took the trail mix from Sam and picked around the raisins. "Where are you thinking?"

Kevin named a lake Kelsey had heard of but never visited, tucked inland enough that people argued about whether the approach was worth it. There was a rough trail in, established camping near the water if you arrived early, and a mountain route above it with views on a clear day.

"Water source?" Kelsey asked.

"Lake, filtered," Kevin said. "Creek on the way in if it has not dried up."

"Bears?"

"Possible. Normal precautions."

Sam sighed. "I love when romance includes food storage protocols."

Kelsey looked at her. "You want romance on a trip where you will be carrying freeze-dried meals and digging catholes?"

"I contain multitudes," Sam said, which had become her answer to everything.

Kevin's eyes met Kelsey's for half a second over Sam's head. Amusement, nothing more.

Kelsey looked away first because there was no reason not to.

The idea of the trip stayed practical for the rest of the hike. Kelsey asked about pack weight, route length, whether the campsite had decent tent pads, and how bad the bugs got near the lake. Kevin answered all of it. Sam contributed by explaining that her personal camping style involved being brave for the first six hours and then demanding soup.

"You are selling this beautifully," Kelsey said.

"Good. Come with us."

The words landed too casually to be planned.

Kelsey looked at Sam. "What?"

"Come with us sometime. Not necessarily this one if Kevin insists on making it a mountain pilgrimage. But one of them."

Kevin was suddenly very interested in tightening the lid on his water bottle.

Kelsey noticed that. She also noticed that Sam had noticed her noticing.

"I have gear," Kelsey said, because that was easier than answering anything else.

"Of course you do," Sam said. "You are competent in a way that makes me feel decorative."

"You are decorative."

"Thank you."

"And terrifying."

"Better."

They moved on before the invitation could become a decision. The trail dipped through shade, then rose along a ridge where the sun hit hard enough to make everyone's conversation thin out. Kevin fell back once to point out a loose section. His hand hovered near Kelsey's elbow but did not touch.

"Step left," he said.

She did, feeling the gravel shift under her boot where her right foot would have landed.

"Thanks."

"No problem."

He moved ahead again.

Sam, several steps in front of them, said without turning, "Everyone saw that Kevin was useful."

"I live to provide trail hazard awareness," he said.

"And chili."

"And chili."

"A rich life," Kelsey said.

The return to the cars left them dusty and hungry. Sam stretched against Kevin's truck with one leg propped on the bumper, making wounded noises whenever anyone looked at her.

"I will survive," she said.

"No one asked," Kevin told her.

"Support me."

He handed her a clean shirt from the back seat. "Emotionally or logistically?"

"Yes."

Kelsey changed her shoes beside her own car and listened to them with the comfortable ache of a good hike settling into her calves. Her shirt clung between her shoulder blades. There was dirt on one shin. She felt used in the best outdoor way.

Before she left, Sam caught her by the wrist.

"Seriously. We should do an overnight sometime."

Kelsey glanced toward Kevin, then back to Sam. "You think I want to listen to you complain in the woods overnight?"

"Yes."

"Correct."

Sam grinned and released her.

At home, Kelsey dropped her pack by the door, showered, and made herself eat something with protein before collapsing on the couch. The house smelled faintly of sunscreen and trail dust because she had not put anything away.

Later, she opened the closet where she kept her camping gear.

The overnight pack was there, a little dusty. So was the lightweight stove she had not used since the previous summer, the headlamp with batteries that might or might not be dead, the dry bags, the sleeping pad rolled too tightly because past Kelsey had been angry at it.

She took inventory without committing to anything.

Tent. Pad. Quilt. Rain shell. Base layers. Water filter that needed testing. Bear hang kit. Socks that had earned trust. Underwear that was practical and absolutely not worth thinking about.

She wrote a list in her phone.

At the top, she typed:

`Possible overnight with Sam and Kevin.`

Then she deleted `possible`.

Then she put it back.

The distinction felt important in a way she could not yet explain.
