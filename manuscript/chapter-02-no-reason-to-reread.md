# Chapter 2 - No Reason To Reread

There was no reason to reread Kevin's messages.

Kelsey knew that, because she had made the argument to herself with the patient tone she used for clients who needed the same question answered three different ways. The messages were about light on the water, a muddy section at Colliery Dam, which socks held up best on wet trails, and whether Sam could be convinced to start a hike before brunch if bribed with cinnamon buns.

Nothing in them required study.

So she studied them.

Not dramatically. She did not lie awake clutching her phone or turn a normal exchange into a private prophecy. She checked them in ordinary gaps: while the kettle boiled, while her laptop restarted, while she waited in line at the grocery store behind a man buying six limes and a single frozen pizza.

Kevin wrote the way he talked. Dry, specific, never pushing too hard. If she asked about a trail, he answered with the information she wanted and one observation she had not asked for.

`South side is less muddy if you cut left after the bridge. Still bring shoes you hate.`

Then, a minute later:

`The ducks are currently winning whatever argument they are having.`

He sent a photo of three ducks in aggressive formation near a fallen branch.

Kelsey answered, `They look organized.`

`They have a five-year plan.`

`Do you?`

The typing dots appeared, vanished, appeared again.

`Survive Sam's attempt to make us do a trail run on Saturday.`

That was another safe thing. Us. Not you and me. The three of them, plus whoever else Sam managed to recruit with threats disguised as enthusiasm.

Kelsey put her phone facedown and finished making tea.

Her house felt too quiet when work ended early. The rooms held the light differently in the late afternoon, stretched gold across the floorboards and the second-hand dining table she used more for folding laundry than eating. She changed into leggings and a windbreaker, tied her hair up badly, and walked down toward the water because sitting still would make the phone too interesting.

The Hammond Bay air had that almost-rain weight to it. Cars hissed over damp pavement. The ocean appeared and disappeared between houses, pewter under a low sky. Kelsey walked until her calves warmed and the restlessness in her chest loosened into something she could name as exercise.

Sam called halfway back.

"Are you walking?" Sam asked.

"No, I always breathe like this while standing still."

"Hot."

"You called to flirt with my cardio?"

"I called to ask if you are coming Saturday. Kevin says the trail will be easy, which means he is lying kindly."

"Where?"

"Colliery. Nothing heroic. We may acquire pastry afterward if you behave."

"If I behave?"

"If I behave. Sorry, I projected."

Kelsey smiled and stepped around a puddle. "Who's going?"

"You, me, Kevin, maybe Dana if she stops pretending she has a family obligation every time elevation exists."

"So probably three."

"Probably three."

There it was again. Three. The shape that kept everything legible.

Saturday was cool and bright, the trails still damp enough that roots shone dark through the dirt. Sam arrived wearing sunglasses too large for her face and a fleece she claimed made her look outdoorsy.

"It makes you look like you stole a youth pastor's camping supplies," Kelsey said.

Sam looked down at herself. "Honestly, that tracks."

Kevin adjusted the strap of his camera bag. "Do we need to unpack that statement?"

"Never unpack before a hike," Sam said. "You lose daylight."

They started along the lake path with Sam between them at first, talking with both hands and occasionally drifting into Kevin's shoulder when the trail narrowed. Kevin carried the heavier pack because he had snacks, extra water, and a small first-aid kit that Sam called his emotional support gauze.

The hike stayed easy. That was its danger. No one had to concentrate hard on footing except in brief muddy patches, so conversation wandered. Sam told a story about a parent meeting. Kelsey complained about the way people said "quick favour" when they meant "unpaid project." Kevin pointed out a cedar with bark twisted like rope, and Kelsey stopped to take a picture with her phone.

"Send me that?" Kevin asked.

She looked at the screen. It was not bad, actually. The light caught the wet bark in uneven lines.

"Why?"

"Because you saw it differently than I did."

Kelsey made a face to cover the small pleasure the sentence gave her. "That sounds like photography nonsense."

"It is the highest category of nonsense."

Sam leaned over Kelsey's shoulder. "I like it. Very tree. Strong bark representation."

"Thank you, art critic."

Kelsey sent the photo to both of them in the group chat before she could make it strange.

Kevin did not compliment it right away. He waited until they had walked another ten minutes and Sam had moved ahead to inspect a signboard about local fish.

"The angle was good," he said.

Kelsey kept her eyes on the trail. "You are just encouraging me because you want more amateur tree content."

"The foreground was messy in a good way. Made the trunk feel taller."

Specific. Again.

She felt the comment settle somewhere it had no business settling.

"Thanks," she said.

He nodded like she had handed him something fragile and he knew better than to squeeze.

After the hike, they got pastry and coffee from a place Sam insisted had the best butter tarts within reasonable driving distance. Sam sat beside Kevin in the booth and stole foam from his latte with a spoon. Kelsey sat across from them, warmed by exercise and sugar, watching them fit around each other without thinking.

Sam's hand on Kevin's wrist. Kevin tearing his croissant in half and giving her the larger piece because he had already learned not to ask. Sam bumping her knee against his under the table when he said something too dry. Their ease was not performative. It had dents in it, old arguments sanded down into habits, private jokes that survived without explanation.

Kelsey loved that about them.

That was why nothing in Kevin's messages mattered.

At home that night, she showered, changed into soft clothes, and opened the group chat to send Sam a picture of the butter tart she had bought for later and then eaten in the car.

Kevin had sent her tree photo back privately.

He had cropped it slightly and adjusted the contrast so the wet bark gleamed and the trail behind it blurred into green.

`You had the shot. It just needed a nudge.`

Kelsey stared at the screen.

Then she typed, `That is annoyingly better.`

`Not better. More itself.`

She read that once.

Then again.

There was no reason to reread it a third time.

She did anyway.
