# Chapter 4 - Sunrise Without Sam

Sam refused the sunrise hike with the moral force of a woman defending civilization.

"Absolutely not," she said from beneath Kevin's duvet when Kelsey arrived at their house in the dark. "The sun has risen without my supervision for years."

Kevin stood in the hallway with a travel mug in one hand and his camera bag over his shoulder. "You said last night you might come."

"Last night I was aspirational."

Kelsey leaned against the doorframe of the bedroom, trying not to smile too hard. The only lamp on was on Sam's side of the bed, turning the room amber and soft. The king mattress nearly touched the walls on both sides. Sam had one bare foot sticking out from under the blankets. Aspen occupied the bottom corner with her head on Kevin's pillow, looking deeply committed to Sam's argument.

"Neck Point will be cold," Kelsey said.

Sam pointed at her without opening both eyes. "You understand me."

"Traitor," Kevin said.

"Historically, yes," Sam murmured, and rolled over.

So it was just Kevin and Kelsey.

The fact did not become strange until they were in the truck.

Kevin had left the engine running to warm the cab. The windows were still fogged around the edges. Kelsey climbed in with her coffee and a backpack that felt excessive for a short walk, but she had packed water, an extra layer, and a granola bar because Kevin's practical habits were apparently contagious.

"She really was going to come last night," Kevin said as he backed out of the driveway.

"No, she wasn't."

He glanced at her.

Kelsey took a sip of coffee. "She was enjoying the idea of being a sunrise person. That is different."

Kevin's mouth curved. "Fair."

The streets were almost empty. Nanaimo before dawn had a held-breath quality, traffic lights changing for no one, porch lights glowing over wet steps, the occasional truck passing with purpose. Kelsey watched the road unwind through the windshield and told herself that sharing a quiet cab with Kevin was not different from hiking with him and Sam, only earlier.

It was different.

Not because he did anything. That was the problem. He drove with both hands relaxed on the wheel, asked whether the heat was too high, and talked about the tide line at Neck Point because he wanted to catch the water low enough to expose the rocks.

Everything he did made the situation more ordinary.

Which made it harder to dismiss the small, alert part of herself that noticed his profile in dashboard light.

At the park, the air was colder than Kelsey expected. It came off the water with teeth in it, cutting through the gaps at her collar and sleeves. She zipped her jacket higher while Kevin checked his camera.

"Still time to go back and join Sam's moral stand," he said.

"I am not letting her win that easily."

"She is asleep. She already won."

They started along the path with their breath showing pale in front of them. The world was mostly shape and sound: dark trees, damp railings, gravel under boots, the steady hush of water moving against rock. Somewhere to their right, a bird made one experimental note and then seemed to think better of it.

Kevin walked slightly ahead where the path narrowed. His camera strap crossed his chest. Every so often he stopped without warning, not dramatically, just pausing because something had caught his eye. Kelsey learned to stop with him.

"What are you looking at?" she whispered once, though there was no reason to whisper except that the dark made normal volume feel rude.

Kevin pointed toward a gap in the trees. "That line."

At first she saw only water and sky, both nearly the same grey. Then the horizon sharpened, and above it a thin seam of orange began to show.

"Oh," she said.

"Yeah."

He lifted the camera, adjusted, lowered it again.

"No picture?"

"Not yet."

She looked at him instead of the horizon. His face had changed. Not softened exactly, but quieted. He was not hunting for a pretty shot to send later. He was waiting for the moment to become itself.

Kelsey understood, suddenly, why his photos felt attended to.

The light widened slowly. Orange became gold at the edge, then pale yellow where the clouds thinned. The rocks below them emerged piece by piece. The tide had left dark ribbons of kelp, small pools reflecting the sky, gulls standing with proprietary disdain on exposed stone.

Kevin moved down carefully from the path to a lower shelf of rock. He offered Kelsey a hand.

She could have managed without it.

She took it anyway.

His fingers closed around hers for two seconds, warm despite the cold. He steadied her over a slick patch and released her as soon as she was balanced.

"Watch the left side," he said. "It is worse than it looks."

"That feels like a metaphor."

"For Sam before coffee, maybe."

Kelsey laughed, and the laugh came out too bright in the quiet morning.

They stood on the rocks while Kevin took photos. He showed her how moving two steps changed the whole frame, how the railing became clutter from one angle and structure from another, how a person did not need to be in a picture to be felt by it.

"That sounds sad," she said.

"Does it?"

"A little."

He considered the water. "I think it sounds generous."

Kelsey looked down at her boots because looking at him felt too direct.

Nothing happened.

That was what made the morning stay with her.

He did not stand too close for too long. He did not make a joke about Sam sleeping and what that left them free to do. He did not use the hour or the empty shoreline or the cold as an excuse to turn toward her in a way she would have to answer.

He took pictures. He explained light. He offered his hand twice more where the rocks were slick, and each time let go before the contact could become a question.

By the time they returned to the truck, the sun had cleared the low cloud and the parking lot was no longer empty. Kelsey's cheeks ached from cold. Kevin's hair had been pushed into disorder by wind.

Sam had texted both of them.

`If the sunrise was ugly, lie to me.`

Kevin sent a photo of the gold line over water.

Sam replied, `I made the right choice. Beautiful from bed.`

Kelsey looked at the picture on her phone and knew it was good.

She also knew it did not contain the best part of the morning.

That stayed unphotographed: Kevin waiting, the cold railing under Kelsey's hand, the brief warmth of his fingers, and the strange new knowledge that nothing happening could feel like a kind of invitation.
