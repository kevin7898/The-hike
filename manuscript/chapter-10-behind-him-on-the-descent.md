# Chapter 10 - Behind Him On The Descent

The next group hike should have made things easier.

Sam was there. Dana actually came after three weeks of claiming rotating obligations. Kevin brought Aspen, which meant at least one creature on the trail was incapable of complicated subtext. The day was bright, the air warm enough for short sleeves after the first climb, and the route had enough people on it to keep privacy from gathering too quickly.

Kelsey told herself all of that while lacing her boots in the gravel parking area.

Sam appeared beside her and held out sunscreen.

"Face," she said.

"Good morning to you too."

"You burn when you pretend clouds are infrastructure."

"That happened once."

"It happened dramatically."

Kelsey took the sunscreen. Sam watched her apply it, then reached out and rubbed a missed streak along Kelsey's cheekbone with her thumb.

"There," Sam said.

The touch was ordinary. They had been friends too long for casual contact to need explanation.

Kelsey still felt it after Sam stepped away.

Kevin was clipping Aspen's leash to his belt and arguing with Dana about whether a granola bar containing chocolate counted as breakfast. He looked relaxed in a faded green shirt, pack high on his shoulders, camera tucked away for once. When his gaze crossed Kelsey's, he smiled without changing his conversation.

That helped.

It did not help enough.

The climb spread them out. Dana set a pace that suggested she intended to prove something to no one but herself. Sam stayed near Kelsey, talking about work, weekend plans, and a show she claimed Kevin had ruined by guessing the plot too early. Kevin moved ahead with Aspen where the trail narrowed, looking back often enough to keep everyone together.

At the viewpoint, they stopped for water and snacks. The city lay below them, roofs and roads softened by distance, the ocean beyond it shining in broken panels of light. Sam sat on a flat rock and leaned back on her hands.

"This is almost worth the climb," she said.

Kevin handed her a water bottle. "High praise."

"Do not get comfortable."

Dana dropped onto the ground with a groan. "If anyone says the word switchback on the way down, I will become violent."

Aspen licked her ear.

"Thank you for your support," Dana told the dog.

The lunch break settled into the easy mess of people who had earned food. Tortillas became wraps. A cherry tomato rolled into the dirt and Sam rinsed it with a solemnity no tomato deserved. Kevin cut cheese with a pocketknife and passed pieces around. Kelsey shared dried mango. Dana accused everyone of pretending trail mix was not just snack confetti.

For a while, the tension thinned into friendship.

Then they started down.

Kevin led because Aspen wanted to move and because he was good at spotting loose gravel before anyone else committed weight to it. Sam followed him, then Kelsey, then Dana, who had declared herself "emotionally rear guard."

Kelsey had spent enough hikes behind Kevin for it not to matter.

It mattered now.

The trail dropped steeply in short, uneven stretches. Kevin bent his knees on the rough parts, one hand out to the side for balance. His shirt pulled across his shoulders when he reached back to steady Sam over a root. Sam took his hand, hopped down, and squeezed his fingers before letting go.

Normal.

Kelsey watched the motion with too much attention.

At the next rough patch, Sam turned sideways and stepped down without help. Kevin glanced back at Kelsey.

"Loose left side," he said.

"I see it."

"Take the middle."

She did, and the gravel slid under her boot anyway. His hand came out. She caught it hard enough to feel the tendons shift under his skin.

"Got you," he said.

Two words. Practical, quiet.

Sam was watching from three steps below.

Kelsey let go and hoped her face looked like a person who had simply avoided falling.

"Graceful," Dana said from behind her.

"I am a mountain goat," Kelsey said.

"A deeply anxious one."

The trail widened after that, allowing Sam to fall back beside Kelsey. For several minutes they walked together under fir shade, the ground softer with needles.

"You okay?" Sam asked.

"Because I almost died in a gravel incident?"

"Because you made your overthinking face."

Kelsey looked ahead at Kevin's back. "You need to stop identifying my faces."

"Develop new ones."

"Impossible."

Sam bumped her shoulder lightly. "You do not have to narrate anything to me before you understand it yourself."

Kelsey looked at her.

Sam's expression was hidden partly by sunglasses, but her mouth was softer than the joke had been.

"I know," Kelsey said.

"Good."

"Do you?"

Sam's smile turned wry. "Not always. But I am trying to be impressive."

Kelsey laughed because the alternative was feeling too much.

They reached a creek crossing near the bottom where the bridge was more ambition than structure: two wide planks slick with damp, a handrail on only one side. Kevin crossed first with Aspen, then turned back.

Dana looked at the planks. "Absolutely not."

"It is four feet," Sam said.

"My objection is spiritual."

Kevin came back across, took Dana's pack to make her feel less top-heavy, and then guided her with the calm seriousness of a man defusing a bomb. Sam crossed after with unnecessary flair. Kelsey waited last.

"Your turn, mountain goat," Kevin said.

She stepped onto the first plank. It bowed slightly.

"Do not make goat jokes while I am vulnerable."

"Noted."

Halfway across, she looked down at the water moving over stones and felt the small dizzy tug of motion beneath stillness. Kevin offered his hand.

This time she needed it.

His grip was firm. She crossed the last steps and landed close enough that her shoulder brushed his chest. Not an accident, not fully on purpose. His hand stayed around hers until she looked up.

There was no time to read him properly because Sam said, "I would like credit for crossing with style."

Kevin released Kelsey and turned. "You made jazz hands."

"Style," Sam insisted.

The rest of the hike returned them to noise: parking lot gravel, dogs barking, packs thumping into trunks, Dana announcing that she had become a new person who respected benches. Sam dug a tube of lip balm from Kevin's pack and used it without asking. Kevin opened water for Aspen. Kelsey changed shoes beside her car and tried to make her body feel like public property again.

Before she left, Sam hugged her.

Not a quick one. A real one, with Sam's cheek warm against hers.

"Still okay?" Sam murmured.

Kelsey closed her eyes for one second.

"Yes."

Sam pulled back. "Good. Text me your gear list later. I want to mock it."

"You want to copy it."

"Both."

Kevin lifted a hand from beside the truck.

"Drive safe, Kels."

She smiled. "You too."

On the way home, her phone lit with a message from him.

`Thanks for the dried mango. Sam is pretending she did not eat most of it.`

Kelsey read it at a red light and smiled.

Then Sam texted separately.

`I ate most of it.`

The triangle was still intact.

That was the thing Kelsey kept telling herself as she drove home with the feel of Kevin's hand still ghosting across her palm and Sam's hug still warm at her shoulder.
