# Chapter 3 - Pictures Back

Kelsey started taking pictures before she admitted she was taking them for Kevin.

At first it was easy to pretend she had simply become more attentive. The spring light changed quickly on the Island. Trails that had looked flat and brown in February were suddenly layered with new green, wet stone, salmonberry flowers, and the glossy darkness of roots after rain. Anyone might notice that. Anyone might stop during a walk and frame the curve of a path because a friend had taught her, accidentally, that the edge of a picture could matter more than the centre.

She sent the first one from Westwood Lake.

Not immediately. She stood under a cedar with her thumb hovering over the screen while two runners passed behind her, their shoes slapping the damp gravel in matching rhythm. The photo showed the trail bending away into shadow, a bright strip of lake visible through the trees. It looked peaceful in a way the actual moment had not been, because a child nearby was loudly refusing a banana.

She almost deleted it.

Kevin's pictures looked intentional. Hers looked like she had stopped walking and pointed her phone at something.

Then she heard Sam's voice in her head, unimpressed and accurate: Kels, if you make sending a tree picture weird, I cannot help you.

Kelsey sent it.

`Trying to prove I can perceive nature.`

Kevin did not answer for fourteen minutes. Kelsey spent nine of those minutes telling herself she did not care and five wondering whether the framing was worse than she thought.

His reply came while she was unlocking her car.

`You caught the trail disappearing. That's the good part.`

Her hand stilled on the door handle.

Another message followed.

`Also excellent nature perception. Very advanced.`

She laughed, relieved enough to resent him for it.

`Do I get a certificate?`

`Sam owns a label maker. We can make this official.`

Kelsey pictured it: Sam at the kitchen table, delighted by the opportunity to label something ridiculous; Kevin pretending to object while enabling her completely. The image was so clear and domestic that it made the private thread feel less private.

That helped.

For the next two weeks, pictures became part of the rhythm. Kevin sent her shoreline rock, a crooked mushroom, Aspen sitting in a patch of sun as if she had personally discovered warmth. Kelsey sent back trails, coffee on her deck, a blurry eagle that Kevin identified as "emotionally an eagle" when she admitted it might be a crow.

They did not text every day. That would have been something else. There were gaps where work swallowed her, where Sam took over the group chat with three unrelated stories in an hour, where Kevin disappeared into early shifts and errands and the quiet machinery of his life with someone else.

Kelsey liked the gaps more than she expected.

They kept the messages from becoming a demand.

One Friday, Sam invited Kelsey over for dinner again and opened the door with wet hair, bare feet, and a towel around her shoulders.

"Kevin is trapped at the grocery store because he trusted a list I wrote while hungry."

"That seems like a couple problem."

"It is now a community problem. Come in."

Kelsey stepped around Aspen, who greeted her with the exhausted joy of a dog who had not seen her in at least four days and had nearly perished from it.

Sam's phone buzzed on the counter.

`They do not have the good tortillas.`

Sam read it aloud with the seriousness of breaking news.

"Tell him to improvise," Kelsey said.

Sam dictated into her phone, "Kelsey says improvise, which means your tortilla failure is now performance art."

Kevin sent back a picture of two competing tortilla brands.

Sam handed the phone to Kelsey. "You choose. I cannot carry this marriage alone."

"You are not married."

"Emotionally, to the tortilla crisis."

Kelsey picked the one with fewer suspicious claims about authenticity and sent the message from Sam's phone.

`Kelsey has spoken.`

Kevin replied, `Then it is binding.`

The words gave her a quick, silly warmth. Because they came through Sam's phone, she let herself keep it.

Kevin arrived fifteen minutes later with groceries, rain in his hair, and the wrong cilantro because he had grabbed parsley while distracted by a man arguing into a headset near the produce section. Sam held up the parsley like evidence.

"This is why you need supervision."

"I was under pressure."

"From herbs?"

Kelsey took the bag from him. Their fingers touched only because the handles shifted. It was nothing. Cold plastic, warm skin, gone before it could decide what it wanted to be.

Kevin did not react.

Kelsey did not either.

Dinner became tacos assembled at the counter because no one wanted to clear the table. Sam leaned against Kevin while he chopped vegetables. Kelsey grated cheese and listened to them bicker about whether parsley had any business being included out of spite.

Later, Sam sprawled on the couch with her feet in Kevin's lap and made Kelsey show them the Westwood Lake photos she had not sent. Kevin took her phone carefully, his thumb moving slower than hers would have.

"This one," he said.

Kelsey leaned over the back of the couch to see which.

It was a picture she had taken without meaning to keep: Sam and Kevin ahead of her on the trail, slightly out of focus, Sam turned toward him mid-sentence, Kevin looking at her like he was about to laugh. The lake behind them had blurred into light.

"That is not a trail picture," Kelsey said.

"No," Kevin said. "It is better."

Sam reached for the phone. "Let me see."

Kelsey handed it over, suddenly self-conscious. "It's blurry."

Sam looked at the picture for a long time. Her smile softened in a way Kelsey rarely saw aimed at herself.

"Send me that," Sam said.

"Really?"

"Yeah."

Kelsey sent it.

Sam saved it immediately.

That night, back in her own house, Kelsey sat on the edge of her bed and opened the photo again. She liked the way Sam and Kevin looked together. She liked that she had seen it. She liked that Kevin had noticed the picture's value before she did.

None of that was dangerous.

Not yet.

Across town, Sam sent a selfie from bed, hair piled messily on top of her head, Kevin's shoulder barely visible beside her and Aspen wedged at their feet with the confidence of a creature paying no rent.

`Your photo has been promoted to fridge candidate.`

Kelsey answered, `High honour.`

Kevin replied from his own phone a minute later.

`It really is a good picture.`

She looked at the words longer than necessary.

Then she put the phone facedown on the nightstand.

The screen lit once more through the thin gap between phone and wood.

She picked it up.

Sam:

`Stop overthinking whatever you are overthinking.`

Kelsey stared, then laughed because Sam knew her too well and not nearly enough.
