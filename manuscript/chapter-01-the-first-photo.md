# Chapter 1 - The First Photo

The photo arrived while Kelsey was standing barefoot on her deck with a mug of coffee cooling in both hands.

Kevin had sent pictures before, usually to the group chat when he and Sam were out somewhere pretty enough to make the rest of their friends pretend not to be jealous. This one came straight to Kelsey.

No caption at first. Just a strip of morning light on wet rock, the ocean beyond it dull silver under cloud, and a line of fir branches cutting across the top of the frame. There was nothing dramatic in it. No sunset. No impossible colour. No person standing in the middle with arms lifted to prove they had been there.

That was why she liked it.

She leaned her hip against the railing and enlarged the picture with her thumb. The boards under her feet were cold from overnight rain. Below her rented house in Hammond Bay, the road curved out of sight between damp hedges and parked cars. The water was visible only in pieces from this angle, broken by rooftops and trees, but the smell of it came up clean and mineral through the morning.

Kevin followed the photo with a text.

`Neck Point was showing off a little.`

Kelsey smiled into her coffee.

`A little?`

`I am trying to sound restrained.`

`It doesn't suit you.`

There was a pause long enough for her to take one careful sip and remember she was texting Sam's boyfriend before work with bare legs and an old hoodie zipped halfway over a sleep shirt.

Then Kevin answered.

`Sam says that exact sentence at least twice a week.`

That made it safe again.

Kelsey typed, `Sam is usually right.`

`Usually?`

`Tell her I said always. I value my life.`

The reply came with a picture of Sam's hand giving a thumbs-up in the passenger seat, one chipped black nail visible, Kevin's dashboard blurry behind it.

`She accepts tribute.`

Kelsey laughed out loud. A gull answered from somewhere down the street like it disapproved of private amusement before eight in the morning.

She saved the first photo without thinking about it, then noticed that she had and told herself it meant nothing. Kevin was good with a camera. That was all. He saw ordinary places in a way that made them feel attended to, and Kelsey liked knowing people who paid attention.

By noon, she had looked at the picture three more times.

Work took most of the afternoon from her in the usual way: messages that needed answers, a call that could have been an email, a spreadsheet that treated every small correction like an accusation. Kevin did not text again. Sam did, sending a voice note during her lunch break about a coworker who had tried to store oat milk in a cabinet and then looked betrayed when gravity got involved.

Sam's voice filled Kelsey's kitchen while Kelsey made toast over the sink.

"Anyway, Kevin is making chili tonight and he says there will be leftovers, which means he has cooked for seven people again. Come over after work if you want to prevent waste."

Kelsey held the phone away from her face and recorded back, "Tell your domestic partner that I support his inability to measure beans."

Sam's response was immediate.

"He heard that and says bring sour cream."

So Kelsey went.

Sam and Kevin's house always looked most like them when no one had tried to prepare it for company. The front mat had two pairs of muddy shoes beside it and Aspen's leash looped over the railing. A stack of Sam's school papers occupied one end of the kitchen table. Kevin's camera bag sat near the hall closet where someone could trip over it with real commitment.

The bedroom door was half-open when Kelsey passed the hall. She saw the corner of the king bed crammed into the small room, blankets already dragged sideways by two people who slept like weather systems. A laundry basket blocked one dresser. On the wall above the bed, Sam had hung a framed print Kevin had taken at Westwood Lake in fog.

The room looked crowded and lived in and completely theirs.

Kelsey looked away before looking became rude.

"Shoes off unless you want Kevin to make his tragic face," Sam called from the kitchen.

"I have never made a tragic face about floors," Kevin said.

Kelsey stepped out of her shoes. "You made one last month when I spilled salsa."

"That was structural grief."

"The tile survived."

"Physically."

Sam appeared in the doorway with a wooden spoon in one hand and a grin that made her eyes narrow. "See? This is why I need you here. He gets dramatic without witnesses."

Dinner was chili, too much cornbread, and Sam stealing the best pieces of bell pepper from Kelsey's bowl when she thought Kelsey was not watching. Aspen stationed herself beneath the table with the moral confidence of a dog who had never been fed in her life.

Kevin asked Kelsey about work. Not with the flat politeness of someone filling space, but with follow-up questions that proved he remembered the last thing she had complained about. Kelsey told him about the spreadsheet. Sam interrupted to defend the honour of bad administrative systems because she enjoyed being wrong when it annoyed Kelsey.

After dinner, Kevin showed Kelsey the photo from that morning on his camera screen. It looked different there. Sharper. Less like a pretty text and more like evidence of a person deciding what mattered.

"The branches make it," Kelsey said.

Kevin glanced at her, pleased in a quiet way. "That is what Sam said."

"I said the branches looked like spooky fingers," Sam said from the couch.

"You said they gave it structure."

"I contain multitudes."

Kelsey leaned closer to the camera. "I like that it is not centred."

Kevin looked at her again. "Yeah?"

"It feels like you were passing by and caught it before it disappeared."

The room went calm around the comment, not awkward exactly, but attentive. Kevin's hand rested on the camera strap. Sam watched them both over the rim of her glass with an expression Kelsey could not read quickly enough.

Then Aspen sneezed beneath the table and ruined any chance of the moment becoming serious.

Kelsey stayed later than she meant to. That happened often at Sam's house. Time softened there, broken into refills, another story, another five minutes leaning in the doorway with her keys already in her hand.

When she finally left, Sam hugged her first, hard and careless.

"Text when you're home."

"It is twelve minutes."

"Text when you're home."

Kevin stood behind Sam with one hand in the pocket of his hoodie. "Drive safe."

"Always," Kelsey said.

Outside, the evening had cooled. Her car smelled faintly of sour cream from the container she had forgotten on the passenger floor until Sam chased her down with it. She started the engine, backed out of the driveway, and saw Kevin and Sam through the front window for one second before the angle changed.

Sam had turned toward him.

Kevin had already reached for her.

Kelsey looked back to the road.

At home, she texted Sam that she had arrived safely. Then, after standing in her kitchen for a minute with the deck door open to the sea air, she opened Kevin's morning photo again.

She told herself it was because she wanted to look at the branches.

For now, that was close enough to true.
