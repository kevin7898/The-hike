# Chapter 13 - Not A Date

"It is not a date," Kelsey said.

Sam, on speakerphone, made a thoughtful sound that contained no agreement.

"It isn't."

"I heard you."

"You heard me and then made a noise like a therapist with popcorn."

"That sounds like an incredible business model."

Kelsey stood in front of her closet wearing jeans and a bra, with three shirts laid across the bed like evidence of a crime. Kevin had offered to meet her at the outdoor store after work because he needed fuel canisters and Kelsey wanted to compare pack liners. Sam was working late. The plan was practical, public, and aggressively unromantic.

Kelsey had changed shirts twice.

"Wear the blue one," Sam said.

Kelsey looked at the bed. "You can't see them."

"I can hear your panic. Blue is usually the answer."

"This is not panic."

"Kels."

"Fine. It is mild wardrobe concern."

"For your not-date."

"I hate you."

"No, you don't. Blue."

Kelsey wore the blue one.

The outdoor store was busy enough to save her and quiet enough to make saving incomplete. Kevin waited near the dehydrated meals with a basket over one arm, reading the back of a package with grave distrust.

"Do not buy that one," Kelsey said.

He looked up. "You don't know which one it is."

"It has the energy of something with powdered eggs."

He turned the package around. "Breakfast skillet."

"See?"

"You have a gift."

She took the package from him and put it back. "I protect my community."

They moved through the store like people with a shared task. Fuel canisters. Pack liners. A new lighter because Kevin did not trust the old one. Bug spray. A headlamp strap to replace one Sam had somehow stretched out, possibly through violence.

Every aisle created a new kind of almost.

Almost brushing shoulders near the water filters. Almost reaching for the same package of wipes. Almost making the obvious joke in front of the camp towels and both choosing not to. Kevin stayed Kevin through all of it: useful, dry, attentive without crowding.

"Do you need anything else?" he asked.

Kelsey looked toward the clothing section before she could stop herself.

"Maybe socks."

"You always need socks."

"That is what I tell myself."

The women's base layers were near the socks, which was poor store planning or excellent psychological warfare. Kelsey ran her fingers over merino shirts, then leggings, then a rack of sports bras in colours designed by people who believed hiking required neon optimism.

Kevin stopped at a respectful distance.

"I can look at knives with emotional intensity if you want privacy."

She laughed. "I am capable of buying socks while supervised."

"Brave."

She chose two pairs and did not look at the underwear display nearby. Not until Kevin turned to compare two small multitools hanging on the end of the aisle.

Then she looked.

Performance fabric. Seamless. Quick-dry. Practical, technically. Some of it still looked like a decision.

She picked up a black pair, then put it down.

Kevin's voice came from behind her. "Sam swears by the brand with the green label."

Kelsey closed her eyes for one second.

"Of course she does."

"That sounded less helpful after I said it."

She turned. His ears were a little red.

Good. Not only her, then.

"Did she send you to advise me on underwear?"

"No. I have just heard more reviews of hiking underwear than any man deserves."

"Poor Kevin."

"I endure."

Kelsey picked up the green-label pair and added it to her basket with as much dignity as possible. Then, because some new part of her had become reckless in public, she added the black pair too.

Kevin looked away.

Not before she caught the flicker.

The rest of the errand had to survive that. It did, barely. At the register, Kevin paid for his items and Kelsey paid for hers. Separate bags. Separate receipts. Nothing that looked like a couple from the outside.

Outside, the evening had cooled. The parking lot smelled like hot pavement losing heat and someone nearby smoking in a parked car.

"Coffee?" Kevin asked.

Kelsey looked at him.

He added, "Or not. It is not a trap."

"That sounds like something a trap would say."

"A trap would offer better coffee."

She should have gone home.

She said yes.

They walked to a cafe two blocks away and sat outside because the weather allowed it. Their shopping bags rested under the table between their feet like neutral witnesses. Kevin ordered black coffee. Kelsey ordered tea and then regretted it because holding a mug with both hands made her feel like a person in a conversation she had not rehearsed.

"Does this feel weird?" she asked.

Kevin's answer came after a pause. "A little."

"Good."

"Good?"

"I was going to be annoyed if you said no."

He smiled. "Then a medium amount."

"Better."

They let the honesty settle.

"I don't want to mess up your life," Kelsey said.

Kevin looked down at his coffee. "You are not an outside force that happens to us."

"That is a very calm sentence."

"I practiced in my head."

Her mouth softened. "Did you?"

"Different versions."

"What were the bad ones?"

"Anything that started with relax."

She laughed. "Wise edit."

He looked at her then. "Sam matters."

"I know."

"I know you know. I need to say it because otherwise I feel like I am sneaking around the most important fact."

Kelsey's throat tightened. "She matters to me too."

"I know."

"I am not trying to take something."

"I don't think you are."

The relief was too large for the size of the conversation. Kelsey looked at the street, at cars passing, at a woman walking a small dog in a sweater despite the mild evening.

"I also don't know that what I want is a relationship," she said.

Kevin stayed very still.

She forced herself to continue. "Not because I don't care. I do. That is part of the problem. But I look at you and Sam, and that is a life. I don't want to try to climb into the middle of it and ask everyone to call it romantic because it is intense."

Kevin's expression changed in a way she could not read quickly.

"What do you want?" he asked.

The question had no pressure in it. That made answering harder.

Kelsey looked down at her hands. "Access."

She laughed once, embarrassed. "That sounds terrible."

"No, it doesn't."

"It sounds like I am requesting a building pass."

"A little."

She smiled despite herself.

Then the smile faded. "I want heat. Trust. Honesty. I want to be allowed to want you without pretending I am auditioning to be your girlfriend."

Kevin took that in with the care he brought to everything dangerous.

"Okay," he said.

"That is not an answer."

"It is the beginning of one."

Her phone buzzed before she could ask for more.

Sam:

`How is the not-date?`

Kelsey stared at the message, then turned the phone so Kevin could see.

He laughed quietly.

Kelsey typed back, `Productive. We bought socks.`

Sam:

`Filthy.`

Kelsey laughed, and Kevin did too, and whatever had opened between them stayed open without needing to be solved that night.

When they parted in the parking lot, he did not hug her. She was grateful and disappointed in equal measure.

At home, she unpacked the socks, the pack liner, the green-label underwear, and the black pair she had chosen because Kevin had looked away too late.

She put both pairs in the camping pile.

Not because it was a date.

Because it was not, and that was starting to matter more.
