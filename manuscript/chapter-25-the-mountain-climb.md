# Chapter 25 - The Mountain Climb

After Sam's text, Kelsey climbed differently.

Not faster. The mountain did not care about emotional breakthroughs, and her legs had limits no amount of filthy encouragement could erase. The trail steepened above the signal rock, crossing loose scree and root steps that required hands as often as feet. Sweat gathered under her pack. Her calves burned. Her breath came harder in the thin, wind-clean air.

But something in her had stopped apologizing.

Kevin noticed.

He had always noticed. The difference was that now, when she caught him looking, she did not rush to rescue them both.

At a water stop below a stand of twisted firs, Kelsey shrugged out of her overshirt and tied it around the strap of her daypack. The shirt beneath clung to her chest and stomach with sweat. She took her time adjusting the hem.

Kevin looked at the trail.

"You can look," she said.

He laughed once, not because it was funny but because it hurt. "You are enjoying this."

"A little."

"Good."

The answer startled her.

"Good?"

He looked back then. "Yes. Good."

The heat that moved through her had nothing to do with exertion.

They kept climbing.

The trail narrowed along a slope of rock and low brush, forcing Kevin behind her for a stretch. Yesterday, that position had made Kelsey self-conscious and restless. Today, she knew exactly what he could see when she leaned forward to use her hands on the rocks, when her shirt lifted at the small of her back, when the line of her leggings moved with each step.

She gave him one view on purpose.

Only one. She stopped above a rock step, braced one hand on her thigh, and looked back as if checking the route. The movement was plausible. The glance was not.

Kevin stood below her with one hand on the rock, breathing hard. His eyes had dropped before she turned and were still climbing back when she caught him.

"Need help?" she asked.

His mouth tightened.

"Cruel."

"I am being trail supportive."

"No, you are not."

She smiled and continued up.

The confidence did not remove fear. It made room beside it. Kelsey still felt the ache of what had changed, the strange tenderness of Sam's messages, the concern that the ease of the morning might become confusion later. But the guilt no longer got to drive every thought. It had to sit in the back with all the other complicated passengers.

At the next open patch, Kevin caught up and touched her elbow.

"Break."

"Bossy."

"Sweaty."

"Also true."

They sat on a sun-warmed rock with the lake far below and shared the last of the salted nuts. Kelsey stretched one leg out and flexed her foot, feeling the pull through her calf and thigh. Kevin watched the movement, then looked away with a smile that suggested he was aware of failing gracefully.

"How bad is the thoughtful walking?" he asked.

Kelsey choked on a nut.

"You cannot ask me that while I am eating."

"Safety note accepted."

"Also you are not allowed to call it that."

"Sam named it."

"Sam named nothing. I panicked in text form."

Kevin grinned, then softened. "Seriously."

She considered the honest answer. "A little sore. Mostly good. Noticeable."

"We do not have to do anything else this trip."

"I know."

"I am saying it anyway."

"I know."

She bumped his boot with hers. "I also know I want to."

His eyes lifted.

"Not right this second," she added. "I am eating trail nuts and my hair is stuck to my neck."

"Devastating conditions."

"Obviously."

"But later?"

Kelsey looked toward the ridge. Wind moved through the low trees, carrying the smell of sun-warmed pitch and stone. Her body, tired and tender, answered before caution did.

"Probably."

Kevin accepted probably like it was precious.

The climb above that point required more work and less talking. The trees thinned into subalpine scrub. Rock warmed under their palms. Kelsey had to concentrate on where she placed her boots. Sweat ran down her spine beneath the pack, and the waistband of her leggings felt damp against her skin.

At a steep section, Kevin went first and then offered both hands from the top.

"This one is not optional," he said.

"I decide what is optional."

"After you get up here alive."

She put her hands in his and climbed. The last step brought her into him harder than expected. His arms closed around her automatically, steadying her against his chest. Her face landed near his throat, where sweat and clean skin and sunscreen made a smell that hit her with unreasonable force.

Neither of them moved.

"Alive," he said.

"Barely."

His hands were at her back. One high between her shoulders, one low where the pack did not cover. The lower hand had no innocent explanation after the first second.

Kelsey lifted her head.

His mouth was close.

"We are in the open," he said.

"I know."

"Trail."

"I know."

"Kels."

She kissed him anyway.

Not long. Not enough to become unsafe on a rock shelf with packs and tired legs. Enough to say she could choose heat without needing perfect privacy, and he could answer without turning every spark into a wildfire.

When she pulled back, his hand remained at her lower back.

"You are trouble," he said.

She smiled. "I learned from Sam."

"That is the most credible thing you have ever said."

They climbed the last stretch with the air between them lighter, dirtier, and more honest. Kelsey still had to stop twice to catch her breath. Kevin still made her drink water. The mountain still demanded attention. Desire did not replace the body doing hard work; it lived inside it, in sweat and muscle and the way Kevin's hand hovered near her when the trail narrowed.

Just before the summit opened, the path passed through a slot between two rocks. Beyond it, sky widened.

Kevin stopped.

"Almost there."

Kelsey stood beside him, breathing hard, cheeks hot, legs tired, body still faintly sore from him and newly alive with anticipation.

"Good," she said.

He looked at her.

She looked back.

"I want the view," she said.

Kevin's smile was slow and not innocent. "Both kinds?"

Kelsey laughed and pushed past him toward the summit.
