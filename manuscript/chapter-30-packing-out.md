# Chapter 30 - Packing Out

The hike out began with the wrong kind of quiet.

Not bad quiet. Not angry. Kelsey knew the difference. This was weight quiet, pack straps and sore legs and the strange compression that happened when a place that had held too much meaning became a trail again.

The first hundred metres climbed away from the lake before dropping into trees. Kelsey refused to look back until the water disappeared behind cedar and salal because she suspected looking back too soon would make her dramatic in a way she did not have energy to defend.

Kevin walked ahead with the ruined tent lashed awkwardly to the outside of his pack.

"It looks like you are carrying a body," she said when they stopped to adjust straps.

"It is the corpse of my competence."

"May it rest in mildew."

"Too soon."

The joke helped. So did the trail's indifference. Roots still needed stepping over. Gravel still shifted. The pack still pressed into Kelsey's hips until her body remembered the rhythm and stopped objecting to every stride. Sweat rose under clean clothes, mixing with the faint smell of lake-safe soap and smoke that would not leave anything they owned.

Every practical sensation tethered her to the day after.

They stopped at the creek where the trail crossed over stones slick with green. Kevin went first, set his pack down on the other side, and came back without being asked.

"I can do it," Kelsey said.

"I know."

He held out his hand anyway.

She took it because both statements were true.

His grip was the same as the hike in and not the same at all. Three days ago, taking his hand had been plausible deniability. Now she knew the shape of those fingers on her hip, her thigh, the back of her neck. She knew how carefully he could hold still. She knew what his restraint sounded like when it finally broke.

Crossing a creek should not have contained all of that.

It did.

At the far bank, he did not let go immediately. Neither did she.

"Still sore?" he asked.

"Yes."

"Bad?"

"No." She smiled faintly. "Thoughtful."

He groaned. "That word is ruined forever."

"Sam's gift to language."

They climbed on.

The hike out was easier in theory because their food was lighter. In practice, the body kept receipts. Kelsey's thighs ached on descents. Her shoulders had hot spots despite adjusting the pack. Kevin's pace slowed under the extra bulk of the tent bag, though he would not admit it until Kelsey threatened to tell Sam he was being heroic and therefore annoying.

At lunch, they sat on a fallen log and ate the last wraps with peanut butter and too much trail grit. Kevin checked the map. Kelsey checked her phone, found no signal, and felt both relief and impatience.

"I want to talk to her," she said.

Kevin folded the map. "Yeah."

"I also want to shower, sleep for twelve hours, and never answer a question again."

"That may conflict with talking to Sam."

"Sam will bring questions to the shower if necessary."

"Accurate."

The thought of Sam waiting at home changed the air again. Not home with Kevin, not exactly. Sam in her own kitchen or theirs, phone nearby, pretending to be casual and failing in ways Kelsey knew by heart. Kelsey wanted to see her so badly it hurt. She feared seeing her almost as much.

"What if she is less okay in person?" Kelsey asked.

"Then we listen."

"You make that sound simple."

"It isn't. It is just the only useful answer I have."

She leaned her shoulder against his for one brief second, pack to pack and sweat to sweat.

"Useful is good," she said.

The final stretch of trail flattened into forest that looked too ordinary for what they were carrying out of it. Day hikers appeared near the last kilometre: a couple with trekking poles, two teenagers in running shoes, a man with a dog who gave Kelsey and Kevin the respectful wide berth reserved for backpackers who smelled like commitment.

Kelsey became aware of public again.

Not ashamed. Not exactly. But aware that the world had categories. Friends. Couples. Strangers. People who knew how to explain why they were walking together with a ruined tent and a private history three days old.

What were they, if someone asked?

Friends with benefits, maybe. Friends who had crossed a threshold. Sam's boyfriend and Sam's best friend. None of those fit into the quick social shapes people used in parking lots.

At the trailhead, the truck appeared too suddenly.

It was exactly where they had left it, dustier now, windshield marked with dried rain and pollen. Ordinary metal. Ordinary road beyond it. The world had the audacity to have waited unchanged.

Kevin set down his pack with a groan.

Kelsey dropped hers beside it and stood with her hands on her hips, feeling lighter and less protected at the same time.

"We made it," he said.

"With the corpse."

"With the corpse."

They unloaded themselves in stages: boots changed, packs lifted into the truck bed, moldy tent sealed as if it might contaminate memory, water bottles found, wet bags checked. Kelsey used a wipe on her face and immediately felt like a more civilized disaster.

Kevin leaned against the tailgate and looked at her.

"What?" she asked.

"I don't want to make the truck weird."

"Too late. It smells like socks and emotional consequence."

He smiled, then sobered. "Do you want space on the drive?"

She knew what he meant. No touching unless she initiated. No pressure to debrief. No assumption that because the woods had held them, the cab should.

"I want quiet," she said. "But not distance."

"Okay."

He closed the tailgate.

Before they got in, Kelsey stepped toward him and wrapped both arms around his waist.

Kevin held her carefully, pack sweat and all. The hug was not sexual. That made it necessary. She pressed her face into his shirt and breathed in smoke, clean soap, effort, him.

"This was not nothing," she said.

His hand moved over her back once.

"No."

"But we still have to figure out what it is."

"Yes."

She let go before the parking lot could start to feel like a stage.

They climbed into the truck, closed the doors, and brought the ordinary world back with the turn of a key.
