# Chapter 16 - Trail In

The trail made bodies honest before either of them had to be.

For the first half hour, Kelsey thought mostly about weight. The pack rode correctly because Kevin had adjusted it in the parking area with brisk, impersonal hands, but correct did not mean light. The hip belt pressed firm around her pelvis. Shoulder straps tugged. Every step announced what she had chosen to carry: food, clothes, shelter, water, the red underwear tucked where no one could see, and the possibility neither of them had named since leaving the truck.

The path began gently, which Kelsey distrusted immediately. It wound through second-growth forest, damp needles underfoot, salal crowding the edges, cedar trunks rising dark and furrowed. Sun came through in pieces. Water clicked somewhere below the trail, hidden by fern and slope.

Kevin walked ahead at first, setting a pace that respected the packs without insulting her. He had a way of checking back without making it look like checking. A pause to adjust his strap. A glance toward a bird call. A stop to point out a place where the trail split around a fallen log.

"Left side is better," he said.

"It looks worse."

"It drains better."

"That is a suspiciously adult answer."

"I have my moments."

She took the left side and did not sink to the ankle in mud, which annoyed her because he was right.

The climb began after the creek.

Not dramatically. No cinematic wall of mountain. Just a steady change in grade that made conversation thin and then disappear. Gravel shifted under boots. Roots crossed the trail at angles designed by someone with a grudge. Kelsey warmed fast, unzipping her outer layer, then stopping ten minutes later to strip it off entirely.

Kevin turned away while she wriggled out from under the pack straps.

She noticed.

"You can look," she said before she could decide whether it was wise.

He looked back slowly.

She had only removed a jacket. She was in a hiking shirt, sports bra beneath it, pack belt still clasped around her hips. Nothing private was visible. Still, his gaze landed with enough restraint to make her feel the shape of what he was not letting himself do.

"I know," he said.

Two words, quiet enough that the forest nearly took them.

Kelsey folded the jacket and tucked it under the top strap of her pack with fingers that felt less coordinated than usual.

They kept moving.

The trail narrowed above the creek into a rough section of exposed roots slick with old rain. Kevin went first, then turned and held out a hand.

"This part actually deserves help."

"Unlike all the other times I took your hand for fraudulent reasons?"

His mouth curved. "Your words."

She put her hand in his.

The weight of the pack changed the contact. She had to trust the pull, step where he indicated, lean into his grip when her boot slid. His palm was warm and dry. His fingers tightened around hers at the exact moment her balance shifted, and her body answered before thought could make it complicated.

She came up onto the higher root shelf too close to him.

Pack to pack, breath to breath.

Kevin did not step away quickly. He did not step closer either.

"Okay?" he asked.

"Yes."

It was too small an answer for the size of what moved through her, but he accepted it because there was still trail ahead and because patience was apparently one of his more dangerous habits.

By late morning, sweat had become part of the day. It gathered under Kelsey's pack, dampened the waistband of her leggings, made loose strands of hair stick to her neck. Kevin's shirt darkened between his shoulder blades. When he stopped for water, he braced one boot on a rock and tipped his head back to drink. Kelsey looked at his throat, then at the trail, then at the water bottle in her own hand as if hydration required moral focus.

"Snack?" he asked.

"Are you asking because I look hungry or because you schedule human maintenance?"

"Both."

They ate standing near a mossy stump. Granola for him, dried mango for her, a shared handful of salted nuts from a bag Sam had labelled in black marker: `FOR EMOTIONAL REGULATION`.

Kelsey held up the bag. "She knows us."

"Unfortunately."

"What did she pack for you?"

Kevin reached into the side pocket of his pack and produced a folded note.

Kelsey stared. "Of course she did."

"I was instructed to open it at camp."

"That is either sweet or a threat."

"With Sam, those categories overlap."

The thought of Sam's handwriting sitting in Kevin's pack made Kelsey's chest ache. Not guilt exactly. Something more complicated and less clean. Sam had stepped back but she had not disappeared. Her love moved with them in ink, in snacks, in the permission that was not permission so much as trust handed over with shaking hands.

The trail steepened again.

Kelsey led for a while because she asked to. She wanted to feel the day from the front, wanted Kevin behind her where she could not keep reading his face. That turned out to be a tactical error. With him behind, every bend, every step up, every place the trail forced her to lean forward became newly vivid. The pack made her shirt ride up at the small of her back. Sweat cooled there when the wind moved. She could feel the muscles in her thighs working, the press of fabric, the knowledge that he could see what she was choosing not to hide.

"Pace okay?" she called back.

"Good."

His voice had roughened with the climb.

She liked that too much.

At the last rise before the lake, the forest opened slightly and the air changed. Cooler. Wetter. A faint open brightness appeared through the trees ahead.

Kelsey stopped without meaning to.

Kevin came up beside her.

Between the trunks, water flashed blue-grey under a broken sky.

"There it is," he said.

The lake looked impossible after the green tunnel of the trail: wide, cold, ringed with rock and fir, the far shore rising toward the mountain they might climb tomorrow if weather and bodies allowed. A raven crossed above the water with one harsh call. Wind moved over the surface in dark patches.

Kelsey forgot, for several seconds, to be nervous.

"Worth the pack?" Kevin asked.

She looked at the lake, then at him. Sweat had dried salt at his temple. His face was open with the pleasure of bringing someone somewhere beautiful and having it work.

"Yes," she said.

Then, because honesty had apparently become contagious, she added, "So far."

He smiled.

They started down toward the campsite with the lake ahead of them and the whole trip finally real beneath their feet.
