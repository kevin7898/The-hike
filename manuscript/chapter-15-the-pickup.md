# Chapter 15 - The Pickup

Kevin arrived six minutes early and looked guilty about it.

Kelsey saw the truck through the front window before he texted. It idled at the curb with the canopy already packed tight behind him, rain beads drying on the windshield from an early shower that had passed before breakfast. She stood inside her entryway with her boots unlaced and one hand pressed to the strap of her pack, waiting for nerves to settle into something useful.

They did not.

Her phone buzzed.

Kevin:

`No rush. I am early because I have a problem.`

She looked at the truck, then at the dry bags lined up beside her door, then down at the hiking clothes she had chosen with too much care. Leggings. Dark green shirt. Sports bra that made her feel held rather than flattened. The black underwear beneath it, because apparently she was now a person who made decisions under base layers.

Sam had texted twenty minutes earlier.

`Remember: you owe nobody performance. Not me, not him, not your anxious little brain.`

Then, a minute later:

`But hydrate. Sluts need electrolytes.`

Kelsey had laughed so hard she nearly dropped her toothbrush.

Now the message sat in her pocket like a charm and a dare.

She opened the door.

Kevin stepped out of the truck when he saw her. He wore hiking pants and a charcoal shirt under an open flannel, hair still damp enough to show he had showered recently. Not helpful. His eyes moved over her once, pack, boots, face. Careful. Present.

"Morning," he said.

"You look like you are about to apologize to my luggage."

"I was thinking through where everything should go."

"Of course you were."

He smiled, but the awkwardness stayed around his mouth. "Sam texted you?"

"Yes."

"Me too."

Kelsey raised an eyebrow. "Do I want to know?"

Kevin pulled out his phone and turned the screen toward her.

Sam:

`Bring her home alive, respected, and only as filthy as she personally chooses.`

Kelsey read it twice. Heat rose up her neck, but beneath it came relief so sharp it almost hurt.

"That is very Sam," she said.

"Yes."

"What did you say?"

"Yes ma'am."

Kelsey laughed. "Coward."

"Strategic man."

The laugh helped them move. Kevin lifted her pack and did not comment on its weight until she did.

"Too much?" she asked.

"Manageable. You packed like you expect weather and feelings."

"Both are possible."

"On Vancouver Island? Unheard of."

They loaded the truck with the precise, unromantic care of people who would later need to find things in the dark. Kelsey's pack went beside Kevin's. Food bin against the left side. Stove kit. Water filter. Tent poles. Sleeping bags in dry sacks. Kevin's extra blanket tucked near the back where Kelsey noticed it and pretended not to.

Before she climbed in, she looked toward her house. The deck was visible above the hedge, empty in the grey morning. She had locked the door, turned off the lights, checked the stove twice, and still felt like she was leaving one version of herself standing inside.

Kevin waited without filling the pause.

"Okay," she said.

They got in.

For the first ten minutes, the drive was all logistics. Ferry traffic they were not taking. Road construction. Weather. Trail conditions. Whether the lake would be warm enough to swim, which Kevin answered with "probably not" and Kelsey interpreted as "yes, if pride is involved."

Then the silence arrived.

It was not empty. It sat between them with Sam's absence clearly shaped inside it. In every other version of the trip, Sam would have been in the passenger seat or leaning forward from the back, narrating snack needs, mocking Kevin's route confidence, turning her own nerves into comedy before anyone else could be nervous. Kelsey felt the missing sound of her like a third person who had chosen not to come and was therefore more present, not less.

"I miss her already," Kelsey said, surprising herself.

Kevin's hands stayed steady on the wheel. "Me too."

That helped more than any reassurance could have.

"Does that make this worse?" she asked.

"No. It makes it honest."

She looked out at the passing trees. "You practiced that answer too?"

"A little."

"It was good."

"Thank you."

The road narrowed as they left town behind. Houses thinned. The truck climbed through green shade and open cuts where logging roads branched off like bad decisions. Kelsey drank coffee she barely tasted. Kevin pointed out the turn they would use on the way back, the creek where the road sometimes washed out, the ridge they might see from the summit if weather held.

His practical voice steadied her.

Her body did not calm.

Every shift of the truck made her aware of the red underwear folded deep in her dry bag, the condoms in the side pocket, the test results exchanged three nights ago with the strange formality of trust made visible. She thought of Sam at her kitchen table saying, Everything else is a choice.

The trailhead appeared around a bend, a gravel pullout with two other vehicles and a weathered sign half-swallowed by salal. Kevin parked, killed the engine, and neither of them moved immediately.

Rain ticked once from a branch onto the windshield.

"Last chance to fake an ankle injury," he said.

Kelsey looked at him. "Is that for me or you?"

"Yes."

She smiled, then let it fade into something truer. "I want to be here."

Kevin's expression changed. The awkwardness did not vanish, but it aligned around that fact.

"Me too," he said.

They stepped out into air that smelled of wet leaves, gravel, and the faint cold mineral scent of water somewhere uphill. Packs came out. Straps were tightened. Kevin checked the truck twice. Kelsey locked her phone into airplane mode, then unlocked it again to send one last message.

To Sam:

`At trailhead. Still okay. Scared in a normal way.`

Sam answered before Kelsey could put the phone away.

`Good. Normal scared means alive. Text when you have signal. Love you.`

Kelsey pressed the phone to her chest for one second, then packed it.

Kevin lifted his pack onto his shoulders. "Ready?"

She bent to tie her boots, buying one last breath.

When she stood, the weight of the pack settled against her hips and shoulders, real and grounding.

"Ready."

They stepped onto the trail together, and the truck disappeared behind trees within thirty paces.
