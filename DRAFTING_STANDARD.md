# Drafting Standard

## Voice

Use close third person, usually through Kelsey, with selective Kevin POV when his restraint, arousal, fear of taking too much, or awareness of Kelsey's feelings matters.

Keep language direct and readable. The prose can be sensual and explicit without becoming ornate. The average reader should not need a dictionary.

Vary names naturally:

- `Kelsey` and `Kels`
- `Kevin` and `Kev`

## Pacing

This is a novel-length slow burn. Do not rush the first several chapters.

Early chapters should spend time on:

- texting;
- ordinary work/life rhythm;
- hiking plans;
- photos and photography talk;
- group hangouts;
- body language that could still be read as friendship;
- Kelsey's private thoughts before she can name them as desire.

## Texting

Texts should feel casual and human. Avoid making every message a confession. Let tension build through timing, small jokes, image sharing, delayed replies, and Kelsey rereading things after she puts the phone down.

## Hiking Detail

Every hike should have its own physical identity. Track:

- weather;
- trail surface;
- incline and footing;
- water, mud, dust, roots, rocks, or gravel;
- sweat, cold air, sun, sunscreen, lake water, and damp clothing;
- pack straps, water bottles, snacks, cameras, and practical pauses;
- who leads, who follows, and what each person can see from that position.

Avoid repeating the same trail conversation in different scenery. Each hike should change what Kelsey and Kevin know about each other.

## Body Language

Analyze both Kelsey and Kevin without overexplaining every gesture.

Useful signals:

- standing closer than necessary;
- offering a hand over an obstacle she could handle alone;
- looking away too late;
- touching a pack strap, camera, bottle, or phone to avoid touching each other;
- Kelsey choosing an angle because she knows he can see her;
- Kevin noticing and deciding whether to name it.

## Clothing And Shower Detail

Clothing and shower scenes should be tactile, not summarized.

Track:

- fabric peeling from damp skin;
- elastic releasing hips, ribs, breasts, thighs, or waist;
- wet underwear becoming heavier or more transparent;
- straps sliding, clasps catching, cotton dragging, leggings rolling, and thongs settling;
- Kelsey's fingers pausing when dressing becomes self-touch;
- the difference between grooming for herself and preparing for Kevin to see her.

For shaving/grooming scenes, keep the focus on body awareness, anticipation, trust, and the decision to prepare for intimacy.

## Explicit Detail

The explicit standard is bodily, specific, and character-driven.

Target the same level of adult intensity and physical specificity as the Alex project, but assistant-authored manuscript files should use local-expansion markers for graphic passages. The surrounding prose should still carry heat, buildup, body realism, aftercare, and emotional consequence so the marker has real context to expand from.

Include:

- saliva, wet skin, sweat, lake water, and sex mess where appropriate;
- Kevin and Kelsey reading each other's reactions instead of repeatedly asking if everything is okay;
- Kelsey becoming more vocal as trust grows;
- Kevin becoming better at what he learns from her body;
- physical aftermath, cleanup, and aftercare;
- direct language when the scene's tone calls for it.

Do not make every explicit scene the same kind of escalation. Use different engines: restraint, discovery, urgency, teasing, tenderness, mess, exhaustion, aftercare, or emotional confession.

## Local Explicit Marker Protocol

Assistant-authored manuscript files should keep explicit sexual passages as local-expansion markers rather than graphic prose.

Use hidden Markdown comments in this format:

`<!-- LOCAL_AI_EXPLICIT: Consenting adults 21+. Expand [scene purpose] here. Preserve [required canon/detail]. -->`

These markers are source-visible but do not show in normal rendered Markdown. They should identify the purpose of the missing explicit material without writing graphic choreography.

The line `How's his cock?` is not a marker. It is story text and must remain verbatim.

## Consent And Continuity

All sexual characters are consenting adults. Keep consent active and clear, especially around firsts and changed boundaries.

For this continuity:

- no-condom expectations are discussed before the camping trip;
- sexual health and testing are part of the pre-trip conversation;
- Kelsey should remain active in choosing what happens;
- Kevin's restraint should feel like effort, not indifference;
- aftercare should match the emotional size of the scene.
- Kevin and Kelsey end as friends with benefits, not a formal romantic couple.

## Sam

Sam is not a plot obstacle or a permission machine. She should feel like a full person with pride, jealousy, compersion, worry, humor, and desire.

When Sam supports Kevin and Kelsey, the writing should still leave room for the cost of that support.

Sam is confidently bi. Kelsey is bi but less settled in it. Their tension should not force an immediate triad; it should deepen the friendship and complicate Kelsey's sense of what she wants.
